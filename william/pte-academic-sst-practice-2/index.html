Transcript

Community tool libraries allow residents to borrow equipment such as drills, ladders and gardening tools instead of buying items they may use only once or twice a year. This can lower household costs and reduce the number of rarely used products stored in homes. The libraries can also bring neighbours together because experienced members sometimes show other people how to use unfamiliar equipment safely. However, shared tools create practical problems. Popular items may be unavailable at busy times, and damaged equipment can injure the next borrower. Staff must inspect, clean and repair every item, which requires money, secure storage and trained workers. A digital booking system can improve access, but it will not help people who have limited internet access or live far from the collection point. Tool libraries are therefore most useful when borrowing sites are easy to reach, reservations are reliable and maintenance is treated as a core service rather than an extra cost. They should also make clear that some electrical, structural or specialist jobs still require a qualified professional. With these conditions, sharing equipment can reduce waste and expense without weakening safety.

Model response

Community tool libraries can lower household costs, reduce the number of rarely used products and help neighbours share practical knowledge. However, popular items may be unavailable or damaged, while inspection and storage are expensive. These schemes work best when booking is reliable, equipment is maintained, borrowing sites are convenient and professional safety advice remains available for difficult jobs.

Compare your answer. Did you keep one benefit, one problem and the important conditions?

Transcript

Remote medical appointments became much more common when clinics needed to reduce face-to-face contact, and many patients have continued to use them. A video or telephone consultation can save travel time, give rural patients faster access to a specialist and make routine follow-up easier for people with limited mobility. It may also help clinicians check whether a treatment is working without asking a patient to spend hours reaching a hospital. However, remote care has clear limits. A clinician cannot examine an injury properly through a poor camera, and important signs may be missed when the sound or internet connection fails. Privacy is another concern because patients may discuss sensitive information at home or through an insecure platform. Remote appointments are safest when clinics decide in advance which cases are suitable, use secure technology and provide clear instructions. Patients must also be able to change to an in-person appointment when symptoms are uncertain or a physical examination is needed. Used in this way, remote care can extend access and make follow-up more convenient, but it should support rather than replace local medical services.

Model response

Remote medical appointments can save travel time, improve specialist access and make routine follow-up easier. However, poor connections, privacy risks and the lack of a physical examination can lead to missed information. Remote care is safest when clinics select suitable cases, use secure technology and allow patients to move to an in-person appointment whenever symptoms are uncertain.

Compare your answer. Did you keep the physical examination as a limit rather than saying remote care is always unsafe?

Transcript

Removing admission charges from museums can make collections available to people who might otherwise decide that a family visit is too expensive. Free entry may attract first-time visitors, encourage schools to return more often and allow local residents to spend a short time in a gallery without feeling that they must see everything at once. Museums can also benefit when a larger and more varied audience supports cafés, shops and membership programmes. However, free entry does not remove the cost of running a museum. Buildings still need security, conservation work and trained staff, while crowded galleries can make the visit less enjoyable and may put fragile objects at risk. Visitor numbers can also be misleading because a busy entrance does not prove that people are learning from the collection. A free-entry policy works best when museums receive stable public funding, manage crowded periods with timed booking and offer clear interpretation for different audiences. Donations, paid exhibitions and commercial services may provide extra income, but they cannot always replace predictable funding. Free entry can widen access, but only when museums protect both the visitor experience and the collections they are responsible for.

Model response

Free museum entry can widen access, encourage school visits and attract a more varied audience. However, museums still face staffing, conservation and crowd-control costs, while high visitor numbers do not guarantee learning. The policy works best when public funding is stable, busy periods are managed and museums protect both the visitor experience and their collections.

Compare your answer. Did you keep free entry as useful under conditions rather than completely good or completely bad?
